This report presents the findings of a seismic isolation feasibility study for the Interstate Bridge Replacement (IBR) program, which will replace the aging I-5 Columbia River crossing between Portland, Oregon and Vancouver, Washington. Three bridge alternatives were evaluated using eigenvalue modal analysis, nonlinear static pushover analysis, and ASCE 7-22 property modification factor (lambda) bounding:

  • Alternative A: Conventional ductile 3-span girder bridge with fixed-base RC columns (baseline)
  • Alternative B: Same girder geometry with Triple Friction Pendulum (TFP) bearings at pier foundations
  • Alternative C: Extradosed cable-stayed bridge with TFP bearings at tower bases
Recommendation: Both isolated alternatives (B and C) achieve Immediate Occupancy performance at both the Design Basis Earthquake (DBE) and Maximum Considered Earthquake (MCE) levels, compared to Life Safety (DBE) and Collapse Prevention (MCE) for the conventional design. Seismic isolation reduces substructure force demands by 66–72% and column drift by over 98%. For a critical lifeline structure carrying 130,000 vehicles per day, the 8–12% cost premium of Alternative B delivers compelling resilience benefits. Alternative C offers the highest performance but at a 22–30% cost premium. We recommend Alternative B as the primary design and Alternative C for further study.

2. Project Background

2.1 Existing Bridge

The existing Interstate Bridge consists of twin steel truss structures built in 1917 (northbound) and 1958 (southbound), spanning 3,538 feet across the Columbia River. Now over 100 years old, the bridge carries approximately 130,000 vehicles per day on six substandard lanes with no shoulders. The structure rests on timber piles driven 70–100 feet into sandy alluvial soils—a foundation system wholly inadequate for modern seismic demands.

Seismic vulnerability assessments have concluded that the existing bridge faces catastrophic failure in a Cascadia Subduction Zone (CSZ) M9 event. The timber pile foundations in liquefiable soils, combined with non-ductile concrete piers and rocker bearings, provide no viable path for seismic retrofit.

2.2 Replacement Program

The IBR program, jointly managed by the Oregon Department of Transportation (ODOT) and Washington State Department of Transportation (WSDOT), will deliver an all-fixed-span replacement bridge with 116-foot navigation clearance, as approved by the U.S. Coast Guard in January 2026. The replacement bridge will accommodate eight travel lanes (three through plus one auxiliary in each direction), light rail transit, a shared-use path, and full shoulders. The estimated deck width is approximately 150 feet.

Current cost estimates range from $12.2 billion to $17.7 billion (2025 dollars), with $2.1 billion in federal funding secured through the USDOT Mega Grant and FHWA Bridge Investment Program. Construction is planned to begin in 2026.

Figure 2.1 — Replacement bridge elevation showing representative 3-span segment (3 x 275 ft)

2.3 Design Standards

The replacement bridge must comply with AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications and, for the seismically isolated alternatives, the AASHTO Guide Specifications for Seismic Isolation Design (4th Edition). The bridge is classified under Seismic Design Category D due to the high seismic hazard and Site Class D soil conditions. Additional reference is made to ASCE 7-22 Chapter 17 for property modification factors governing isolation bearing properties.

3. Seismic Hazard

3.1 Cascadia Subduction Zone

The Cascadia Subduction Zone (CSZ) extends approximately 620 miles from northern California to southern British Columbia, posing the dominant seismic threat to the bridge site. Paleoseismic studies have documented 41 great earthquakes (M8.0–9.2) over the past 10,000 years, yielding an average recurrence interval of approximately 243 years. The most recent event occurred on January 26, 1700—over 326 years ago.

A CSZ megathrust event would produce ground shaking of extraordinary duration (2–5 minutes), far exceeding the 10–30 second duration of typical crustal earthquakes. This long-duration demand is particularly damaging to conventional ductile structures, which accumulate damage through many inelastic cycles. The Portland Hills Fault and East Bank Fault also contribute to local seismic hazard as near-fault crustal sources.

3.2 Site Conditions

The bridge site is characterized by soft alluvial soils deposited by the Columbia River, classified as Site Class D to D/E. Liquefaction is expected to depths of 50–60 feet under 500–1,000 year return period events. The existing timber pile foundations, set in these liquefiable soils without reaching bedrock, exemplify the vulnerability of structures not designed for modern seismic standards.

3.4 Significance for Isolation Design

The seismic hazard characteristics at this site make an exceptionally strong case for seismic isolation. The long-duration CSZ ground motions (2–5 minutes) subject conventional ductile columns to hundreds of inelastic cycles, accumulating damage far beyond what is considered in standard pushover analysis. Isolation bearings, by keeping the substructure elastic, eliminate this cumulative damage vulnerability entirely. Furthermore, the heavy superstructure (approximately 45,000 kips for the 3-span segment) provides ideal conditions for friction pendulum isolation, as the large gravity load ensures reliable bearing performance and self-centering capability.

4. Description of Alternatives

4.1 Alternative A: Conventional Ductile Bridge

The baseline design is a 3-span continuous concrete box-girder bridge with 275-foot spans (825 feet total for the representative segment). Each pier consists of a group of four 7-foot diameter reinforced concrete columns, designed for plastic hinge formation at the column bases and tops per AASHTO SDC D requirements. The 150-foot wide deck carries eight lanes plus light rail, resulting in a total tributary weight of approximately 45,000 kips for the 3-span segment.

Key parameters: f'c = 6,000 psi (columns), A706 Grade 60 rebar, pier height = 40 ft (480 in), elastic lateral stiffness = 433.4 kip/in (both piers combined), plastic moment capacity = 271,800 kip-in per pier group.
Figure 4.1 — Alternative A elevation showing plastic hinge zones at column ends (ductile design)

4.2 Alternative B: TFP Isolated Bridge

Alternative B uses the same superstructure geometry as Alternative A but introduces Triple Friction Pendulum (TFP) bearings at the pier foundations, decoupling the superstructure from the substructure. The columns are designed to remain elastic under seismic loading, with 25% higher moment of inertia (Ix = 565,500 in4 vs. 452,400 in4) to resist the transmitted bearing forces without yielding.

TFP Bearing parameters: Reff = 157.5 in (outer surface), friction μfast = 0.08 (outer), 0.02 (inner), displacement capacity = 23.6 in, characteristic strength Qd = 1,166 kips, post-yield stiffness Kpost = 95.24 kip/in.
Figure 4.2 — Alternative B elevation showing TFP bearings at pier foundations (columns remain elastic)

4.3 Alternative C: Extradosed Bridge with TFP Isolation

Alternative C uses an extradosed cable-stayed configuration with spans of 250–500–250 feet (1,000 feet total). The two towers rise approximately 90 feet above the deck, with stay cables providing additional stiffness and redundancy. TFP bearings are placed at the tower bases, isolating the entire superstructure-and-tower system from the foundations. The total tributary weight is approximately 52,000 kips, reflecting the heavier tower and cable system. This configuration reduces the number of in-water foundations from two piers to two towers, offering environmental benefits.

Figure 4.3 — Alternative C elevation showing extradosed configuration with TFP bearings at tower bases

4.4 TFP Bearing Detail

The Triple Friction Pendulum (TFP) bearing used in Alternatives B and C consists of four concave sliding surfaces arranged in a nested configuration. The inner surfaces (small radius, low friction) accommodate service-level movements, while the outer surfaces (large radius, higher friction) engage during seismic events to provide period shift and energy dissipation.

5. Analysis Methodology

5.1 Eigenvalue Modal Analysis

Free-vibration analysis was performed on all three models by solving the generalized eigenvalue problem [K - ω2M]{φ} = 0, where K is the stiffness matrix, M is the mass matrix, ω is the circular frequency, and {φ} is the mode shape vector. For the isolated models, the bearing stiffness was represented using the secant stiffness at the design displacement (Keff) to obtain linearized periods appropriate for spectral demand estimation.

5.2 Nonlinear Static Pushover Analysis

Displacement-controlled pushover analysis was performed using a linear load pattern (forces proportional to mass and height at deck-level nodes). The analysis tracked:

  • Base shear vs. control-node displacement (capacity curve)
  • Plastic hinge formation sequence and locations
  • Bearing displacement demand vs. capacity (isolated alternatives)
  • Column/tower force demand-to-capacity ratios

5.3 TFP Bearing Model

The TFP bearings were modeled using the bilinear hysteretic force-displacement relationship defined by:

  • Characteristic strength: Qd = μeff × W (friction force at zero displacement)
  • Post-yield stiffness: Kpost = W / Leff (gravity-restoring stiffness)
  • Effective period: Teff = 2π√(W / Keff × g) where Keff = Kpost + Qd/D

Design displacement was determined through iterative convergence of the equivalent linearization procedure per AASHTO Guide Specifications for Seismic Isolation Design.

5.4 Property Modification Factors (Lambda)

Upper and lower bound analyses were performed per ASCE 7-22 Section 17.2.8.4 to account for variability in bearing properties due to aging, contamination, temperature, and velocity effects:

The lower bound (λ = 0.85) reduces friction, producing longer periods and larger displacements (governing displacement design). The upper bound (λ = 1.80) increases friction, producing shorter periods and higher forces (governing force design).

7. Cost-Benefit Discussion

7.1 Initial Construction Cost

Alternative B adds an estimated 8–12% to the superstructure cost through the procurement of TFP bearings, but partially offsets this through reduced column reinforcement requirements (elastic design vs. ductile detailing) and potentially smaller foundation elements due to the 66% reduction in substructure force demands. Alternative C carries a higher premium of 22–30% due to the extradosed tower and cable system, but eliminates one in-water pier foundation, reducing environmental impact and marine construction risk.

7.3 Lifecycle Value

For a critical lifeline structure carrying 130,000 vehicles per day and serving as the primary freight corridor for the West Coast supply chain, the economic impact of extended bridge closure after an earthquake would be measured in billions of dollars. A 6–12 month closure of this I-5 crossing would disrupt regional and national commerce, emergency response capabilities, and daily commuter traffic for over 300,000 people. The 8–12% cost premium for seismic isolation in Alternative B—potentially $1–2 billion on a $12–18 billion project—is a fraction of the avoided economic losses from a single major earthquake.

8. Conclusions & Recommendations

Primary Recommendation: Adopt Alternative B (TFP Isolated Girder Bridge) as the preferred seismic design strategy for the Interstate Bridge Replacement. The 8–12% cost premium delivers transformative seismic performance: Immediate Occupancy at both DBE and MCE levels, zero structural damage, and minimal post-earthquake downtime for this critical lifeline crossing.

Secondary Recommendation: Advance Alternative C (Extradosed + TFP Isolation) for further study as a potential premium alternative. While the 22–30% cost premium is substantial, the reduced environmental footprint (fewer in-water foundations), longer main span, and highest seismic resilience may justify the investment, particularly given evolving seismic hazard understanding and the bridge's planned 100+ year service life.

8.1 Summary of Key Findings

  1. Both isolated alternatives achieve Immediate Occupancy performance at both DBE and MCE levels, compared to Life Safety (DBE) and Collapse Prevention (MCE) for the conventional ductile design.
  2. Substructure force demand is reduced by 66–72%, enabling elastic column/tower design and eliminating the need for plastic hinge detailing.
  3. Column drift ratios are reduced by 98%+, virtually eliminating post-earthquake structural damage.
  4. TFP bearing displacement demands are well within capacity (D/C = 0.34–0.36 at DBE), providing substantial reserve for beyond-design-basis events including the long-duration CSZ motions.
  5. Upper/lower bound lambda analysis confirms robust isolation design across the full range of bearing property modification factors (D/C ≤ 0.41 at the most demanding combination).
  6. The 2–5 minute duration of CSZ ground motions particularly favors isolation, as conventional ductile columns would accumulate cumulative damage through hundreds of inelastic cycles—a failure mode entirely eliminated by isolation.
  7. For a critical lifeline structure, the modest cost premium provides a compelling return through near-elimination of earthquake damage and post-event downtime.
